Abstract

Mobile users favor handset (earpiece) mode for private communication, relying on physical sound isolation to protect privacy. In this paper, we challenge this assumption by presenting , the first system to demonstrate that commodity mobile accelerometers can recover intelligible speech from the minute vibrations of the earpiece. Unlike prior works restricted to high-amplitude loudspeaker scenarios, we reveal that extremely weak signals still preserve recoverable linguistic information. To address the challenges of low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and limited sampling rates, we leverage paradigms from the Voice Conversion (VC) domain to propose a practical eavesdropping framework. By extracting linguistic feature from earpiece vibrations, reconstructs intelligible speech without any vocabulary limit and remains effective against unknown victims. Our prototype, strictly within zero-permission constraints, operating extensive evaluations on the LibriSpeech 360-hour dataset (925 speakers) and 14 real-world volunteers. Despite the low-SNR constraints of earpiece mode, achieves average Word Error Rate (WER) of 37.7% for unseen victims. Furthermore, real-world experiments confirm the attack's practicality, maintaining effectiveness across 4 diverse environmental noise levels, 4 distinct phone placements, and 6 heterogeneous device models. We highlight this vulnerability to advocate for stricter operating system countermeasures.
