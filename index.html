Abstract

Mobile users commonly rely on smartphone earpieces for highly sensitive communications, assuming that physical sound isolation effectively protects conversational privacy. In this paper, we challenge this assumption and present VibWhisper, the first system to demonstrate that zero-permission mobile accelerometers can recover intelligible speech from the weak vibrations generated by smartphone earpieces. Unlike prior attacks that rely on high-amplitude loudspeaker leakage and often require prior knowledge of the victim’s device or voice characteristics, VibWhisper shows that attackers can perform eavesdropping without any prior information about the target. Despite the extremely low signal strength, earpiece-induced vibrations still preserve sufficient linguistic information for intelligible speech recovery. To address the challenges posed by low signal-to-noise ratios and limited sensor sampling rates, we propose a practical eavesdropping framework that enables victim- and device-independent semantic extraction under open-vocabulary settings. Extensive evaluations on the LibriSpeech train-clean-360 corpus (921 people) and 14 real-world volunteers show that VibWhisper achieves an average Word Error Rate (WER) of 37.7\% on unseen victims, while remaining robust across diverse real-world conditions. These findings expose a critical vulnerability in current mobile sensor permission policies and underscore the urgent need for stronger operating-system-level countermeasures against such side-channel attacks.
